Overview

Hello everyone, Welcome back to my weekly trash of the week blog. This week I have some very important things to share with you. These items have been very useful to me and that is why I do this blog anyways. I do this because I want to keep track of all the things that have helped me in one way or another. By keeping track of these things I can come back later to visit what I have been doing for each week. This project is similar to Bogost's ideas on an ontography project.

As I work on creating a website to document my environmental footprint and track my trash output, I can't help but notice the similarities and differences between my project and an ontography. Ian Bogost's writing on ontography describes a practice that accounts for the various units that exist throughout the universe. In a similar way, my website is focused on mapping out and understanding the entities that make up my personal waste output. However, while my website and ontography share some similarities, there are also important differences to consider.

One key difference is the scope and comprehensiveness of the two projects. While my website is focused on tracking and reducing my personal waste output, ontography is a broader practice that accounts for all the units that make up the universe. My website may be able to provide insight into my individual environmental impact, but it cannot address the larger systemic issues that contribute to environmental harm. Ontography, on the other hand, aims to account for all units, including those that may be invisible or overlooked, and reveal the underlying complexity of the world.

Another difference between my website and ontography is their respective goals. While my website is practical and action-oriented, ontography is a theoretical and philosophical practice. My website aims to help me take concrete steps towards reducing my environmental impact, while ontography seeks to reveal the rich variety of being and understand the relationships between entities. These different goals highlight the tension between practical action and philosophical inquiry, and prompt us to consider how these different approaches can be integrated to achieve a more comprehensive understanding of environmental issues.

Despite these differences, there are also important similarities between my website and ontography. "ontography can take the form of a compendium, a record of things juxtaposed to demonstrate their overlap and imply interaction through collocation" (Bogost 38). Both practices involve mapping out and understanding the entities that make up a particular system. In my case, this system is my personal waste output, while in ontography the system is the universe. Both practices also seek to reveal underlying relationships and interactions between entities. My website may not be as all-encompassing as ontography, but it still aims to shed light on the relationships between the various entities that make up my waste output.

Furthermore, the similarities and differences between my website and ontography prompt important questions about the role of personal action in addressing environmental issues. As he says, "The practice of ontography and it is a practice, not merely a theory describes the many processes of accounting for the various units that strew themselves throughout the universe" (Bogost 50). On the one hand, my website is focused on individual behaviors, which may seem insufficient in the face of larger systemic problems. On the other hand, ontography highlights the importance of accounting for all units, including those that may be invisible or overlooked. These different approaches prompt us to consider how personal action can be integrated into larger systemic change, and how we can account for the various units that contribute to environmental harm.

In conclusion, the practice of ontography and my website documenting my environmental footprint share some similarities, such as the mapping out and understanding of entities, and some differences, such as their respective scopes and goals. While my website may not be as comprehensive as ontography, it still seeks to shed light on the entities and relationships that make up my personal waste output. The similarities and differences between the two practices prompt important questions about the role of personal action in addressing environmental issues, and highlight the tension between practical action and theoretical inquiry. Ultimately, the integration of these different approaches may be necessary to achieve a more comprehensive understanding of environmental issues and to drive meaningful change.
